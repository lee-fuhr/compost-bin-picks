Compost Bin questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best compost bin depends on your space, volume of kitchen scraps, and whether you want fast results. For most households, a medium-sized stationary bin or a tumbling composter offers a good balance of convenience and affordability. Keep reading for answers to the most common compost bin questions.

What size compost bin is right for me?

Size is the first question most shoppers ask because it directly affects how much waste you can divert. A compact bin that holds roughly a few cubic feet works well for a single person or couple who mainly compost fruit and vegetable scraps. Families with a yard and larger volumes of kitchen waste plus garden clippings will want a bin in the mid-range to larger side, often around ten cubic feet or more. Think about how often you want to empty the bin. A smaller bin fills faster but may need more frequent processing. A larger bin gives you more buffer room for layering browns and greens but takes up more physical space. Most homeowners find that a model in the medium range provides enough capacity without being an eyesore or difficult to turn.

Should I get a tumbling compost bin or a stationary one?

Tumblers are a popular choice because they make aerating the pile as easy as giving the drum a spin every few days. That increased oxygen speeds up decomposition, so you can get finished compost in a matter of weeks rather than months. The trade-off is cost, tumblers tend to be a bit more expensive than stationary bins of a comparable volume, and their rounded shape limits how much material they can hold. Stationary bins, like those made by Geobin or the classic wire-mesh style, offer much more capacity for the price and are simpler to build or assemble. They rely on manual turning with a pitchfork or aerating tool, which takes more physical effort. If you have the space and don’t mind a slower process, a stationary bin can handle a wider variety of materials and larger volumes without breaking the bank.

What material compost bin lasts longest?

Plastic bins dominate the market because they’re lightweight, never rust, and are relatively affordable. High-quality UV-stabilized plastics like those used by brands such as Tumbleweed can last many years, though cheaper models may become brittle after seasons of sun exposure. Plastic bins are also easy to move around the yard. Metal bins, usually made from galvanized steel or powder-coated steel, are very sturdy and can last for decades if kept from rusting. They’re heavier and more expensive. Wooden bins look natural in a garden but will eventually rot, especially in wet climates. Cedar and other rot-resistant woods extend their life, but wood bins typically need replacement sooner than plastic or metal. For best longevity in most climates, a quality plastic bin is the practical choice.

How do I keep my compost bin from smelling?

A healthy compost pile should smell earthy, not foul. Odors usually come from too much moisture or too many nitrogen-rich ‘green’ materials (kitchen scraps, grass clippings) without enough carbon-rich ‘browns’ (dried leaves, cardboard, straw). The fix is simple: add more browns and mix the pile to introduce air. Location matters too. Place your bin on bare soil in a spot with good drainage, and avoid direct full sun that can dry it out. If you’re composting indoors, use a small countertop bin with a charcoal filter or a sealed bokashi bucket. Regular turning and a balanced mix of materials will keep smells at bay in almost any bin.

Can I compost indoors without a backyard?

Absolutely. Apartment dwellers and those without outdoor space have several effective options. A small countertop compost bin with a charcoal filter can hold a few days’ worth of scraps before you transfer them to a larger system. For full processing, you might choose a bokashi bucket, which ferments kitchen waste including meat and dairy, or a worm bin like the Worm Factory that relies on red wigglers to break down scraps into nutrient-rich castings. Worm bins are quiet, odor-free when managed correctly, and can live under a sink or in a closet. Bokashi systems are also compact and produce a fermented pre-compost that can be buried in soil. Both methods allow you to compost year-round with no backyard needed.

Frequently asked questions

What can I put in a compost bin?

Most fruit and vegetable scraps, eggshells, coffee grounds, tea bags, yard trimmings, and shredded newspaper are excellent. Avoid meat, dairy, oily foods, and diseased plants if you’re using a basic backyard bin, these attract pests and cause odors. Bokashi or worm bins can handle a wider range of foods.

How long does it take for compost to be ready?

It depends on the method. Hot composting in a tumbler with the right balance can yield finished compost in as little as three to four weeks. Cold composting in a stationary bin may take six months to a year. Turning the pile regularly and maintaining moisture speeds things up significantly.

Do I need to add worms to my compost bin?

No, you don’t, outdoor compost piles rely on naturally occurring microbes and insects. If you’re using a worm bin specifically designed for vermicomposting, then yes, you need red wigglers. For standard backyard bins, the worms will show up on their own if you place the bin on soil.

Can I compost in winter?

Yes, but decomposition slows down as temperatures drop. Insulating your bin with a layer of straw or moving it to a slightly sheltered spot can help. A pile that is large enough (at least a few cubic feet) retains heat better. Some people keep a small indoor worm bin running through the cold months.

What is the difference between hot and cold composting?

Hot composting requires a balance of greens and browns, frequent turning, and a pile size large enough to generate internal heat. It breaks down materials quickly and kills weed seeds and pathogens. Cold composting is simpler, just pile materials and let nature take its time, but it takes much longer and may not reach temperatures high enough to kill seeds.

In shortChoosing the right compost bin doesn’t have to be complicated. Focus on your space, the volume of scraps you generate, and how much effort you’re willing to put into turning. A well-chosen bin will pay for itself in rich soil and less trash, and you’ll be doing the planet a favor at the same time.
